\chapter{Background}
\label{ch:background}

Here, we introduce the concepts required to understand the CT forward problem, its missing-data regimes, variance-exploding diffusion priors, the PaDIS patch construction method and the reconstruction algorithms used for comparison. For the unacquainted reader, a more comprehensive review of CT fundamentals is given \href{https://github.com/THartigan/MPhil-Project/blob/main/CT_Basics.md}{here}.

\section{Computed Tomography}
\label{sec:basics}

X-ray computed tomography (CT) reconstructs a map of linear attenuation from transmission measurements acquired around an object \cite{guntoro_x-ray_2019}. For a ray path $L$, the Beer--Lambert model gives
\begin{equation}
I=I_0\exp\left(-\int_L \mu(\mathbf{r})\,ds\right),
\label{eq:beer_lambert}
\end{equation}
where $I_0$ and $I$ are the incident and detected intensities. A negative logarithm converts their corrected ratio into a line integral. For a discretised attenuation image, $\mathbf{x}$, the acquisition produced by many projection angles (views) and many offsets is then
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}=A\mathbf{x}+\boldsymbol{\eta},
\label{eq:ct_forward_model}
\end{equation}
where $A$ is the CT forward operator, $\mathbf{y}$ is the sinogram, and $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ is measurement and modelling error. If $A$ is poorly conditioned or has a large null space, many images can explain nearly the same data.

\subsection{Acquisition Geometry and Angular Undersampling}
\label{subsec:ct_geometry_sampling}

Parallel-beam CT uses parallel rays at each view and is the geometry used for the main experiments of Hu et al. \cite{hu_learning_2024}. Fan-beam rays instead diverge from a finite source towards a line detector. This is closer to clinical acquisition and is the geometry used here \cite{wunderlich_image_2008}.

Each parallel-beam view samples a radial line of the object's Fourier transform. Fan-beam sampling differs, but the same intuition applies. Fewer views leave angular gaps and produce streak artefacts, while limited-angle acquisition omits a complete sector and creates direction-dependent ambiguity \cite{sidky_accurate_2006,frikel_characterization_2013}. Learned priors can fill these gaps, but may also add plausible structure unsupported by the measurements. Image quality must therefore be considered alongside measurement consistency.

\subsection{Dose, Noise and Display}
\label{subsec:ct_noise_display}

Dose can be reduced by using fewer views or photons, but these create different inverse problems. Using fewer views increases angular undersampling, whereas using low photon counts increases signal-dependent noise. With $C$ the detected photon count, $C_0$ the incident count, and $P=-\log(I/I_0)$ the ideal line-integral projection from the Beer--Lambert model above, photon detection is approximately Poisson \cite{chang_modeling_2017},
\begin{equation}
C\sim\operatorname{Poisson}\!\left(C_0e^{-P}\right),
\label{eq:poisson_photon_count}
\end{equation}
so log-transformed projections become noisy and biased at low counts. Like Hu et al. \cite{hu_learning_2024}, we use simulated noiseless sinograms and therefore test missing angular information rather than realistic low-photon-dose performance.

CT values are commonly displayed in Hounsfield units (HU) \cite{abutaha_assessing_2021},
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{HU}=1000\frac{\mu-\mu_{\mathrm{water}}}{\mu_{\mathrm{water}}},
\label{eq:hounsfield_units}
\end{equation}
for which water is approximately $0$ HU and air $-1000$ HU. The processed LIDC-IDRI images used here are normalised to $[0,1]$. Figures displayed in HU then use the simple mapping $\mathrm{HU}=3000x-1000$.
\section{Diffusion Models}
\label{sec:diffusion_models}

\subsection{Score-Based SDEs and Variance-Exploding Models}
\label{sec:score_based_sdes}

If we can use a diffuson model to predict score terms, then they can be used to approximate the noisy log-density gradient needed in inverse-problem denoisers and samplers. 

To analyse these score-based vaiants, we consider the continuous-time limit, allowing the noising process can be represented by the stochastic differential equation

\begin{equation}
d\mathbf{z}=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{z},\tau)d\tau+g(\tau)d\mathbf{w},
\label{eq:general_sde}
\end{equation}

where $\tau\in [0,1]$ denotes the continuous diffusion time, and $\mathbf{w}$ is a Wiener process. Anderson and Song \cite{anderson_reverse-time_1982,song_score-based_2020-1} have shown that where $p_\tau(\mathbf{z})$ denotes the marginal distribution of the noisy latents,

\begin{equation}
p_\tau(\mathbf{z})=\int p_\tau (\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})p_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{x})\, d\mathbf{x},
\label{eq:marginal}
\end{equation}

the reverse-time process is

\begin{equation}
d\mathbf{z}=\left[\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{z},\tau)-g(\tau)^2\nabla_{\mathbf{z}}\log p_\tau(\mathbf{z})\right]d\tau+g(\tau)d\bar{\mathbf{w}},
\label{eq:reverse_time_sde}
\end{equation}

where time is integrated backwards and $d\bar {\mathbf{w}}$ is the reverse-time Wiener process. Therefore, once the score, $\nabla_\mathbf{z} \log p_\tau(\mathbf{z})$ is known, the noising process can be approximately reversed, facilitating sampling of the learned image distribution.

The choices of $\mathbf{f}$ and $g$ in \eqref{eq:general_sde} define different families of SDEs \cite{song_score-based_2020-1}. In a variance-preserving (VP) SDE, the signal is attenuated while noise is added according to

\begin{equation}
d\mathbf{z}=-\frac{1}{2}\beta(\tau)\mathbf{z}\,d\tau+\sqrt{\beta(\tau)}\,d\mathbf{w},
\label{eq:vp_sde}
\end{equation}

where $\beta(\tau)$ is a continuous noise-rate schedule. This construction keeps variance bounded for standardised data, but changes the image scale across time.

In contrast, variance-exploding (VE) SDEs are parameterised directly by a noise scale $\sigma$, and add Gaussian noise with increasing variance, such that a noisy image at noise level $\sigma$ is given by

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_\sigma=\mathbf{x}+\sigma\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I}),
\label{eq:ve_noisy_image}
\end{equation}

so the marginal probability to use in the reverse-time process becomes

\begin{equation}
p_\sigma(\mathbf{z})=\int \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{z};\mathbf{x},\sigma^2\mathbf{I})p_{\mathrm{data}}(\mathbf{x})\,d\mathbf{x}.
\label{eq:ve_marginal}
\end{equation}

A denoiser conditioned on this noise level, $D_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\mathbf{z}, \sigma)$, can then be trained to recover clean images from their noisy versions using the loss

\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{VE}}(\boldsymbol{\phi})=
\mathbb E_{\mathbf{x},\sigma,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}
\left[
\lambda(\sigma)
\left\|D_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\mathbf{x}+\sigma\boldsymbol{\epsilon},\sigma)-\mathbf{x}\right\|_2^2
\right],
\label{eq:ve_denoising_loss}
\end{equation}

where $\lambda(\sigma)$ is a weighting over noise scales which can be used to ensure losses at all scales contribute similarly. As the best predictor of a random variable under squared-error loss is its conditional expectation, this also implies that the optimal denoiser is such that

\begin{equation}
D^*(\mathbf{z},\sigma)=\mathbb E[\mathbf{x}\mid \mathbf{z}].
\label{eq:optimal_denoiser_conditional_mean}
\end{equation}

Therefore, using Tweedie's formula and the denoising-score identity \cite{efron_tweedies_2011,vincent_connection_2011} gives

\begin{align}
\nabla_{\mathbf{z}}\log p_\sigma(\mathbf{z})&=
\frac{\mathbb E[\mathbf{x}\mid \mathbf{z}]-\mathbf{z}}{\sigma^2}\\
\implies \mathbf{s}_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\mathbf{z},\sigma)&=
\frac{D_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\mathbf{z},\sigma)-\mathbf{z}}{\sigma^2},
\label{eq:score_from_denoiser}
\end{align}

allowing denoising models to be used in both reverse-VE-SDE samplers and inverse-problem methods as a prior score.

\subsection{The Suitability of Variance-Exploding Models for CT Imaging}
\label{subsec:suitability_ve_ct}

The VE diffusion approach is a natural fit for medical imaging inverse problems because the addition of Gaussian noise to form latent images preserves the units of the underlying images. In contrast, VP noising processes both attenuate the image and add noise.

A VE-denoised prediction, $D_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\mathbf{z},\sigma)$, can therefore be interpreted as a clean-image estimate, enabling direct application of medical imaging measurement models. For example, in CT reconstruction, the corresponding sinogram is predicted as $AD_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\mathbf{z},\sigma)$, where $A$ is the CT forward operator \cite{song_solving_2021,chung_score-based_2022,chung_diffusion_2022}.

\subsection{EDM Preconditioning} \label{subsec:edm_preconditioning}

EDM preconditioning \cite{karras_elucidating_2022-1} embeds a denoising model within a scale-dependent framework rather than using it directly. It wraps a raw neural network, $F_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$, with input, output and skip coefficients to form a preconditioned denoiser. This gives the raw network a better-conditioned learning problem because inputs have a similar scale across noise levels, and the network only needs to predict the part of the denoising map not already captured by the skip connection. For a noisy image $\mathbf{z}$ at noise level $\sigma$, this gives

\begin{equation} D_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\mathbf{z},\sigma) =
c_{\mathrm{skip}}(\sigma)\mathbf{z} + c_{\mathrm{out}}(\sigma)
F_{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \left( c_{\mathrm{in}}(\sigma)\mathbf{z},
c_{\mathrm{noise}}(\sigma) \right), \label{eq:edm_preconditioned_denoiser}
\end{equation}

where

\begin{align} c_{\mathrm{in}}(\sigma) &=
\frac{1}{\sqrt{\sigma^2+\sigma_{\mathrm{data}}^2}}, & c_{\mathrm{skip}}(\sigma)
&= \frac{\sigma_{\mathrm{data}}^2}{\sigma^2+\sigma_{\mathrm{data}}^2}, \\
c_{\mathrm{out}}(\sigma) &=
\frac{\sigma\sigma_{\mathrm{data}}}{\sqrt{\sigma^2+\sigma_{\mathrm{data}}^2}}, &
c_{\mathrm{noise}}(\sigma) &= \frac{\log \sigma}{4},
\label{eq:edm_preconditioning_coefficients} \end{align}

and $\sigma_{\mathrm{data}}$ is an estimate of the standard deviation of
clean training images. The coefficient $c_{\mathrm{in}}$ rescales the noisy
input before it is passed to $F_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$, while $c_{\mathrm{noise}}$
converts the noise level to a logarithmic scale.

The skip connection allows the denoiser to make only small adjustments at low noise levels, whereas at high noise levels, $c_{\mathrm{skip}}$ becomes small, so the denoised estimate relies more heavily on the learned network output. The coefficient $c_{\mathrm{out}}$ then scales this output so that it has the appropriate magnitude for the current noise level. Once the wrapped denoiser $D_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$ has been formed, a score estimate can still be given by \autoref{eq:score_from_denoiser}.

\subsection{The PaDIS Approach}
\label{subsec:padis_approach}

Instead of learning to denoise complete images, PaDIS learns a diffusion prior from image patches, alongside their positional information. This aims to reduce both the memory and data requirements for training, with single images providing many training examples \cite{hu_learning_2024}.

Let $G_c$ be an operator which extracts a patch, $c$, of size $P$ from an image $\mathbf{x}$ of size $N$, along with the positional encoding for the patch, $\boldsymbol{\rho}_c$, before adding noise to only the image part. The positional encoding is formed by scaling the $x$ and $y$ coordinates of the image pixels between $-1$ and $1$. A noisy patch can then be denoted as

\begin{equation}
G_c\mathbf{x}+\sigma\boldsymbol{\epsilon},
\qquad
\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I}).
\label{eq:noisy_patch}
\end{equation}

Using this, Hu et al. \cite{hu_learning_2024} follow the Patch Diffusion 
\cite{wang_patch_2023-1} approach to variable-size, coordinate-conditioned patch training to define the training objective,

\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{PaDIS}}(\boldsymbol{\phi})
=
\mathbb E_{\mathbf{x},c,\sigma,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}}
\left[
\left\|
D_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(G_c\mathbf{x}+\sigma\boldsymbol{\epsilon},\boldsymbol{\rho}_c,\sigma)
-
G_c\mathbf{x}
\right\|_2^2
\right],
\label{eq:padis_denoising_objective}
\end{equation}

which according to \eqref{eq:score_from_denoiser} gives the patch score,

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{s}_{\boldsymbol{\phi},c}(G_c\mathbf{z},\boldsymbol{\rho}_c,\sigma)
=
\frac{
D_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(G_c\mathbf{z},\boldsymbol{\rho}_c,\sigma)
-
G_c\mathbf{z}
}{\sigma^2}.
\label{eq:padis_patch_score}
\end{equation}

Where $k=\left\lfloor N/P \right\rfloor$, an $N\times N$ image could be covered by a $(k+1)\times(k+1)$ grid of non-overlapping $P\times P$ patches after padding. Naively fixing this patch partition and combining the corresponding patch scores would enable construction of a whole-image score, but would cause visible patch boundaries because the patch boundaries remain fixed throughout sampling. To mitigate this, the PaDIS approach zero-pads each image on all sides by

\begin{equation}
M=(k+1)P-N,
\label{eq:padis_padding}
\end{equation}

giving a padded image of size $(N+2M)\times(N+2M)$. PaDIS then considers many different non-overlapping patch partitions, indexed by shifts

\begin{equation}
(i,j)\in\{0,\dots,M-1\}^2,
\label{eq:padis_shifts}
\end{equation}

where each choice of $(i,j)$ also implicitly defines the remaining outer border region from zero-padding. This adds flexibility to the model by allowing different shifts to be sampled during reconstruction, reducing fixed-boundary artefacts.

Using the shifted-patch construction above and the definition of $p_\sigma$ in \autoref{eq:ve_marginal}, the full-image distribution at noise level $\sigma$ can be modelled as

\begin{equation}
p_\sigma(\mathbf{z})
=
\frac{1}{Z_\sigma}
\prod_{i,j}
p_{\sigma,i,j,B}(\mathbf{z}_{i,j,B})
\prod_{r=1}^{(k+1)^2}
p_{\sigma,i,j,r}(\mathbf{z}_{i,j,r}),
\label{eq:padis_full_image_distribution}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{z}_{i,j,r}$ denotes the $r$th patch under shift $(i,j)$, $\mathbf{z}_{i,j,B}$ denotes the bordering zero-padded region, and $Z_\sigma$ is a normalising constant. Differentiating the logarithm of this distribution then gives the whole-image score as a sum of patch scores.

\begin{equation}
\nabla_{\mathbf{z}}\log p_\sigma(\mathbf{z})
=
\sum_{i,j}
\left(
\mathbf{s}_{\sigma,i,j,B}(\mathbf{z}_{i,j,B})
+
\sum_{r=1}^{(k+1)^2}
\mathbf{s}_{\sigma,i,j,r}(\mathbf{z}_{i,j,r})
\right).
\label{eq:padis_whole_image_score_sum}
\end{equation}

The PaDIS implementation fixes the padded border at zero and omits its score, while the denoising network learns the patch terms. Computing all partitions at each step would be prohibitively expensive, so sampling and reconstruction instead select one random shift and use a stochastic whole-image score estimate. For shift $(i,j)$, this gives

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{PaDIS}}(\mathbf{z},\sigma)
\approx
\sum_{r=1}^{(k+1)^2}
G_{i,j,r}^T
\mathbf{s}_{\boldsymbol{\phi},i,j,r}
(G_{i,j,r}\mathbf{z},\boldsymbol{\rho}_{i,j,r},\sigma),
\label{eq:padis_score_estimate}
\end{equation}

where $G_{i,j,r}$ extracts the $r$th patch under the selected partition and $G_{i,j,r}^T$ places the corresponding patch score back into the full image. The boundary artefacts are then instead removed through the iterations, as different random shifts are used at different sampling steps. The flexibility of this prior and its ability to approximate the whole-image score allow it to be used with any standard diffusion sampling and inverse-problem method. In the original paper, Hu et al. found that combining diffusion posterior sampling (DPS) and Langevin dynamics worked best for their CT reconstructions \cite{hu_learning_2024,chung_diffusion_2022,song_generative_2019}. Here, that method is referred to as both VE-DPS and PaDIS.

\section{Reconstruction Methods}
\label{sec:reconstruction_methods}

\subsection{Filtered Back-Projection and the FDK Method}
\label{subsec:fbp_fdk}

Filtered back-projection (FBP) reconstructs parallel-beam data by applying a ramp filter to each projection before back-projecting it through the image. The filter compensates for the over-representation of low spatial frequencies, making FBP fast and interpretable when angular sampling is dense. Sparse views instead produce characteristic streaks because the missing angular information cannot be recovered by filtering alone \cite{sidky_accurate_2006,jin_bone-induced_2012}. Fan-beam and cone-beam analogues additionally weight projections for the divergent ray geometry \cite{feldkamp_practical_1984}.

\subsection{Iterative Reconstruction and Denoising Priors}
\label{subsec:iterative_reconstruction_denoising_priors}

Iterative reconstruction formulates the task as a data-fitting problem rather than a single analytical operation. An initial image estimate is repeatedly updated to improve agreement between its forward projection and the measured sinogram \cite{beck_fast_2009}. For a forward model

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}=A\mathbf{x}+\boldsymbol{\eta},
\label{eq:iterative_forward_model}
\end{equation}

this often involves minimising the squared residual,

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathbf{y}}(\mathbf{x})
=
\frac{1}{2}
\|\mathbf{y}-A\mathbf{x}\|_2^2,
\label{eq:data_loss}
\end{equation}

The gradient of this loss is

\begin{equation}
\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathbf{y}}(\mathbf{x})
=
A^T(A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}),
\label{eq:data_loss_gradient}
\end{equation}

where $A^T$ denotes the adjoint, or back-projection, operator. This enables data-consistency updates of the form

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}^{k+1}
=
\mathbf{x}^k
-
\delta_{\mathrm{data}}
A^T(A\mathbf{x}^k-\mathbf{y}),
\label{eq:data_consistency_update}
\end{equation}

which locally reduce the projection mismatch. The scaling of the update step, $\delta_\text{data}$, however, cannot be chosen independently of the forward operator because the scale of $A$ depends on the reconstruction geometry and image normalisation. The operator Hessian gives the residual curvature,

\begin{equation}
\kappa=\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}^2\mathcal{L}_{\mathbf{y}}(\mathbf{x})
=
A^TA,
\label{eq:data_loss_hessian}
\end{equation}

so the gradient is Lipschitz continuous with Lipschitz constant $L=\|A^TA\|_2$. For the pure quadratic loss term, the resulting Landweber iteration is stable when \cite{hansen_discrete_2010}

\begin{equation}
0<\delta_{\mathrm{data}}<\frac{2}{\|A^TA\|_2},
\label{eq:data_step_stability}
\end{equation}

where 

\begin{equation}
\|A^TA\|_2
=
\max_{\Delta\mathbf{x}\neq 0}
\frac{
\|A^TA\Delta\mathbf{x}\|_2
}{
\|\Delta\mathbf{x}\|_2
}.
\label{eq:operator_norm_definition}
\end{equation}

Because $A^TA$ is symmetric and positive semi-definite \cite{golub_matrix_2012},

\begin{equation}
\|A^TA\|_2
=
\lambda_{\max}(A^TA)
=
\|A\|_2^2,
\label{eq:operator_norm_relation}
\end{equation}

so an operator-normalised form of the update is

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}^{k+1}
=
\mathbf{x}^k
-
\frac{\zeta}{\|A\|_2^2}
A^T(A\mathbf{x}^k-\mathbf{y}),
\label{eq:scaled_data_consistency_update}
\end{equation}

with $0<\zeta<2$ giving stability \cite{hansen_discrete_2010}. The spectral norm can be estimated by power iteration \cite{golub_matrix_2012}, enabling the appropriate scaling of gradient steps by the reciprocal their Lipschitz constant \cite{beck_fast_2009}. Although this does not guarantee convergence once additional priors, denoisers or sampling steps are introduced, it does give the measurement-gradient part of the update the correct scale. This scaling is particularly important in CT because $\|A\|_2$ depends on the projection geometry, detector spacing, number of views, image resolution and measurement units. Therefore, without it, a specific data-consistency coefficient loses meaning across different setups.

This squared-error term alone is normally insufficient in sparse-view CT because many images provide approximately correct measurement projections. Iterative reconstruction is therefore often combined with another regularising term, $R(\mathbf{x})$, such that

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{x}}
=
\arg\min_{\mathbf{x}}
\frac{1}{2}
\|\mathbf{y}-A\mathbf{x}\|_2^2
+
\lambda R(\mathbf{x}).
\label{eq:regularised_reconstruction_objective}
\end{equation}

Often, a total-variation regulariser is used \cite{rudin_nonlinear_1992}. With $D_x$ and $D_y$ denoting the horizontal and vertical finite-difference operators, it is defined as

\begin{equation}
R(\mathbf{x})=\mathrm{TV}(\mathbf{x})
=
\sum_i
\sqrt{
(D_x\mathbf{x})_i^2
+
(D_y\mathbf{x})_i^2
},
\label{eq:total_variation_regulariser}
\end{equation}

favouring piecewise-smooth images while retaining sharp edges -- approximately matching the expected form of CT images.

Chambolle--Pock (CP) solves this non-smooth objective by writing it in the equivalent primal--dual form \cite{chambolle_first-order_2011}

\begin{equation}
\min_{\mathbf{x}}\max_{\mathbf{p},\,\|\mathbf{q}_i\|_2\leq\lambda}
\langle A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y},\mathbf{p}\rangle
+\langle D\mathbf{x},\mathbf{q}\rangle
-\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{p}\|_2^2.
\label{eq:chambolle_pock_saddle}
\end{equation}

Here, $D\mathbf{x}=(D_x\mathbf{x},D_y\mathbf{x})$, the auxiliary dual variable $\mathbf{p}$ represents the projection residual, and $\mathbf{q}$ represents the image gradient. Maximising over $\mathbf{p}$ recovers the squared data-fidelity term, and the pixelwise constraint on $\mathbf{q}$ recovers $\lambda\mathrm{TV}(\mathbf{x})$. CP alternates updates of $\mathbf{x}$, $\mathbf{p}$ and $\mathbf{q}$, using $A^T$ and $D^T$ to map the dual updates from projection and gradient space back into image space. This avoids the ADMM geometry-dependence, so the same formulation applies to different CT operators.

Unlike these explicit regularisers, plug-and-play methods use a learned denoiser within an iterative algorithm \cite{chan_plug-and-play_2017}. PnP-ADMM alternates between a data-fitting estimate $\mathbf{x}$ and a denoised estimate $\mathbf{v}$. It also maintains $\mathbf{u}$, which accumulates the differences between the two estimates and enters updates with opposing signs to compensate for persistent differences. Given $\mathbf{v}^k$ and $\mathbf{u}^k$, the next data-fiting estimate is obtained from

\begin{equation}
(A^TA+\eta I)\mathbf{x}^{k+1}
=
A^T\mathbf{y}+\eta(\mathbf{v}^k-\mathbf{u}^k),
\label{eq:pnp_data_update}
\end{equation}

followed by

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{v}^{k+1}=D_\sigma(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}+\mathbf{u}^k),
\qquad
\mathbf{u}^{k+1}=\mathbf{u}^k+\mathbf{x}^{k+1}-\mathbf{v}^{k+1},
\label{eq:pnp_denoising_update}
\end{equation}

where $D_\sigma$ removes noise at scale $\sigma$. The parameter $\eta$ controls the strength of agreement between the two estimates, and the conjugate gradient method is used to approximately solve the linear system of \autoref{eq:pnp_data_update}.

\subsection{Diffusion Models for Inverse Problems}
\label{subsec:diffusion_models_inverse_problems}

As discussed in \secref{sec:score_based_sdes}, a variance-exploding denoiser provides an estimate of the score. At a fixed noise level, $\sigma_k$, this estimate defines a Langevin update \cite{song_generative_2019} to an existing reconstruction $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}^k$ as

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}^{k+1}
=
\tilde{\mathbf{z}}^k
+
\frac{\alpha_k}{2}
\mathbf{s}_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}^k,\sigma_k)
+
\sqrt{\alpha_k}\boldsymbol{\xi}^k,
\qquad
\boldsymbol{\xi}^k\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I}).
\label{eq:Langevin_step}
\end{equation}

Here, the score term moves the reconstruction towards regions of higher probability under the learned image prior, while the stochastic term maintains sampling diversity.

Reconstruction combines this prior update with a data-consistency update by inserting a gradient step against the measurement residual,

\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{z}}^{k+1}
=
\mathbf{z}^k
+
\delta_{\mathrm{data}}
A^T(\mathbf{y}-A\mathbf{z}^k),
\label{eq:langevin_data_update}
\end{equation}

followed by at least one Langevin prior step from \eqref{eq:Langevin_step}. Here, the data step guides the image towards consistency with the measured sinogram, and the Langevin step pulls it towards the learned image prior. This procedure is termed Langevin reconstruction throughout.

Diffusion posterior sampling (DPS) instead applies the sinogram measurement model to the denoised estimate \cite{chung_diffusion_2022}. Defining the clean-image estimate as

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0^k
=
D_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\mathbf{z}^k,\sigma_k),
\label{eq:dps_clean_estimate}
\end{equation}

the data-consistency update is

\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{z}}^{k+1}
=
\mathbf{z}^k
-
\frac{\zeta_k}{2}
\nabla_{\mathbf{z}^k}
\|\mathbf{y}-A\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0^k\|_2^2,
\label{eq:dps_data_update}
\end{equation}

which can then be followed by at least one Langevin prior step as in \eqref{eq:Langevin_step}. This is generally referred to as the VE-DPS solver throughout this work, with the prior type also quoted. When the prior is patch-based, each step chooses random patch-image offset indices $(i,j)$ and uses the PaDIS combined patches score from \autoref{eq:padis_score_estimate}, matching the PaDIS solver description of Hu et al. \cite{hu_learning_2024}. This convention is used by all diffusion-based inverse solvers in this work.

Discretising the reverse-time SDE \eqref{eq:reverse_time_sde} gives an alternative prediction step from noise level $\sigma_k$ to $\sigma_{k+1}$,

\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{z}}^{k+1}
=
\mathbf{z}^k
+
(\sigma_k^2-\sigma_{k+1}^2)
\mathbf{s}_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\mathbf{z}^k,\sigma_k)
+
\sqrt{\sigma_k^2-\sigma_{k+1}^2}\boldsymbol{\xi}^k.
\label{eq:pc_predictor_step}
\end{equation}

This can then be followed by correction towards regions of higher probability under the learned image prior using the Langevin step from \eqref{eq:Langevin_step}. This procedure is known as the predictor-corrector (PC) method \cite{song_score-based_2020-1}.

Instead of descending the square residual, DDNM methods \cite{wang_zero-shot_2022-1} impose measurement consistency on the denoised $\hat {\mathbf{x}}_0^k$ image using an approximate inverse of the forward operator. Denoting the FDK-based approximate inverse by $B$, the correction takes the form

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{y}}^k
=
B\mathbf{y}
+
(I-BA)\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0^k.
\label{eq:ddnm_measurement_consistency}
\end{equation}

Here, the first term gives the component determined by the sinogram, and the second acts to preserve the component of the denoised estimate lying approximately within the null space of the forward model. Within the VE implementation, this can also be converted back to a score estimate via

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{DDNM}}(\mathbf{z}^k,\sigma_k)
=
\frac{
\hat{\mathbf{x}}_{\mathbf{y}}^k-\mathbf{z}^k
}{\sigma_k^2}.
\label{eq:ddnm_score_estimate}
\end{equation}

The reconstruction is then updated at least once using the Langevin step from \eqref{eq:Langevin_step}, where $\mathbf{s}_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(\tilde{\mathbf{z}}^k,\sigma_k)=\mathbf{s}_{\mathrm{DDNM}}(\mathbf{z}^k,\sigma_k)$. A separate noise-scale coefficient, $\gamma_{\mathrm{noise}}$, can multiply the stochastic Langevin term as $\gamma_{\mathrm{noise}}\sqrt{\alpha_k}\boldsymbol{\xi}^k$, controlling injected sampling noise without changing the denoiser noise level $\sigma_k$ or the score step.
